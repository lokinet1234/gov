The Case for Due Process

An analysis of prosecutorial misconduct, malicious prosecution, and abuse of process.

Chapter 1: Introduction

This case presents a clear pattern of prosecutorial misconduct, malicious prosecution, and abuse of process stemming from the plaintiff’s deliberate concealment of exculpatory evidence and manipulation of legal proceedings.

The defendant’s constitutional rights—under both federal and California law—were systematically violated through a calculated campaign of misrepresentation that perverted the justice system for personal gain.

Context: The California Family Law Act of 1970

This act abandoned a fault-based divorce regime to eliminate perjury and adversarial fault-finding. The very adversarial, fault-seeking tactics now used by the plaintiff mirror the disfavored practices the 1970 reforms sought to eradicate.

1.1

Prosecutorial Misconduct and Brady Violations

Legal Framework

Under Brady v. Maryland, the prosecution bears an absolute constitutional duty to disclose all material exculpatory evidence to the defense, regardless of whether it is specifically requested. This obligation extends to any evidence that tends to establish the defendant’s innocence, supports a defense, or impeaches the credibility of key prosecution witnesses.

Application to Present Case

The defendant’s documented prior reports of abuse by the plaintiff constitute quintessential Brady material. This evidence is material because it provides an alternative causation for the events, undermines plaintiff credibility, and directly supports affirmative defenses such as self-defense.

Authorities: Brady v. Maryland (1963) 373 U.S. 83; People v. Harrison (2017); People v. Kasim (1997); People v. Pettie (2017); Duncan v. Ornoski (2008).

1.2

Malicious Prosecution

A successful malicious prosecution claim requires proof of: (1) favorable termination of the prior proceeding, (2) absence of probable cause, (3) malice, and (4) damages.

Lack of Probable Cause & Demonstration of Malice

The plaintiff’s selective presentation of evidence—cherry-picking text messages while deliberately concealing the defendant’s documented abuse reports—fundamentally undermines any claim of probable cause. Malice is evident through this calculated pattern of deception, mischaracterization of normal communication as harassment, and strategic timing of legal actions to maximize prejudice.

Authorities: Franklin Mint Co. v. Manatt, Phelps & Phillips, LLP (2010); Smith v. Liverpool & London & Globe Ins. Co. (1895); Casa Herrera, Inc. v. Beydoun (2004).

1.3

Abuse of Process

Abuse of process occurs when legal proceedings are perverted to serve an ulterior or improper purpose, even when the initial filing was facially lawful. The essence of this tort lies in the misuse of legal process for illegitimate ends.

Plaintiff’s Ulterior Motives

The evidence demonstrates that the plaintiff’s true objective was not legitimate protection but rather securing the defendant’s arrest as a form of retaliation, gaining strategic advantage in related disputes, and weaponizing the protective order to chill the defendant’s access to court.

Authorities: Loomis v. Murphy (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 1149; S.A. v. Maiden (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 27; Maleti v. Wickers (2022).

1.4

Due Process and Free Speech Violations

Article 1, Section 2(a) of the California Constitution provides: "Every person may freely speak, write and publish his or her sentiments on all subjects, being responsible for the abuse of this right. A law may not restrain or abridge liberty of speech or press."

As Justice Blackmun’s dissent in Feiner v. New York emphasizes, police officers may not silence a speaker simply because they “ask,” “tell,” or “command” him to stop. A lawful speaker must be given courtesy and an explanation of any order to cease.

The prosecution’s reliance on one-sided evidence and failure to investigate the defendant’s prior abuse reports constitutes a violation of due process under the Fourteenth Amendment.

Authorities: Johnson v. Contra Costa County Clerk Recorder (2021); Hernandez v. City of Napa (2011).

1.6

Specific Procedural Violations

The plaintiff’s conduct demonstrates a systematic pattern of procedural violations designed to deny the defendant fundamental due process rights.

Violation of Notice Rights

Plaintiff petitioned for two full trials before defendant had any knowledge of proceedings, violating the fundamental right to notice and opportunity to be heard.

Evasion of Service

Despite defendant’s six-month good-faith effort to resolve disputes, plaintiff ignored all attempts, failing to exercise reasonable diligence in service.

Improper Evidence

Plaintiff introduced 300 new pieces of evidence at the third trial without notice or leave of court, violating rules of civil procedure.

Denial of Hearing

The avalanche of evidence forced in-camera review, denying direct communication and violating procedural fairness.

Conclusion

The plaintiff’s systematic concealment of exculpatory evidence, misrepresentation of communications, misuse of protective-order proceedings, and deprivation of notice and hearing violate federal and California constitutional rights.

These measures subvert due process and resurrect adversarial fault-finding contrary to the 1970 no-fault reforms. Remedies are required to correct this injustice and deter the transformation of legal protections into weapons against lawful speakers and litigants.
