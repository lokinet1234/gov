Malicious Prosecution & Concealment of Evidence

A claim for malicious prosecution arises when legal action is initiated for an improper purpose and without probable cause. The plaintiff's selective presentation of evidence—presenting misleading half-truths while concealing exculpatory facts—vitiates any claim of probable cause.

Core Principle: The Half-Truth as a Lie

Defendants, having undertaken to provide some information, are obliged to disclose all other facts that materially qualify the limited facts disclosed. As one commentator notes, "half of the truth may amount to a lie if it is understood to be the whole."

— Prosser & Keeton, The Law of Torts (5th ed. 1984)

“Moral turpitude” may be inferred not only from violent acts but also from a sustained pattern of deceitful or malicious behavior—such as filing false claims, manipulating institutions, or using legal processes for retaliation. In this case, it is the petitioner—not the respondent—who demonstrates such conduct.

People v. Castro (1985)Randi W. v. Muroc Joint Unified School Dist. (1997)

Procedural & Due Process Violations

The conduct of the hearing in this matter fell far short of providing a meaningful opportunity to be heard. This is a "Kafkaesque" proceeding, a term courts use to describe proceedings "totally lacking in notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard."

Jurisdictional Defect

The presiding judicial officer, a Commissioner, lacked jurisdiction to render a binding decision without an affirmative stipulation from both parties, which was never sought nor given.

Lack of Notice & Vague Allegations

The plaintiff initiated hearings without proper notice, relying on general comments without dates, times, or places—insufficient for a cause of action and too distant to be relevant.

No Meaningful Inquiry

The court made no inquiry into disputed facts or the basis for the defendant's objections. It is not possible that the court could reasonably have made any credibility determinations based solely on the pleadings alone.

Because the right to procedural due process is "absolute," its denial should be actionable for nominal damages without proof of actual injury, even if the underlying claims were justified.

Bd of Regents of State Colleges v Roth, 408 US 564People v. Berch (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1105

First Amendment & The Freedom to Dissent

"The freedom to challenge official action without risking arrest distinguishes a free nation from a police state." This protection is paramount, even when speech provokes a hostile reaction.

"In the realm of religious faith, and in that of political belief, sharp differences arise... To persuade others to his own point of view, the pleader, as we know, at times, resorts to exaggeration, to vilification... But the people of this nation have ordained in the light of history, that, in spite of the probability of excesses and abuses, these liberties are, in the long view, essential to enlightened opinion and right conduct."— Cantwell v. Connecticut

To justify prohibition of speech, the state must show its action was caused by something more than a mere desire to avoid the discomfort and unpleasantness that always accompany an unpopular viewpoint. There must be reasonable ground to fear that serious, imminent evil will result if speech is practiced.

The Risk of Free Speech

A governmental policy of unfettered communication of ideas does entail dangers. To the Founders, the benefits derived from free expression were worth the risk.

The Heckler's Veto

If police can interfere with a lawful public speaker, they must first make all reasonable efforts to protect him. Otherwise, the police become an instrument for the suppression of unpopular views.

Substantive Defenses & Systemic Failures

Several legal defenses apply where the actions of the accused were a direct result of the alleged victim's own conduct or misrepresentation of facts. The system's response often fails to account for these complexities.

The "Violent Victim" Rule

This rule allows a defendant to introduce evidence of the alleged victim’s character for violence to show they were likely the aggressor, forcing the defendant to resort to self-defense.

— People v. DelRio (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 47

Mistake of Fact

A person cannot be charged with a crime if they made a reasonable and honest mistake about the facts, as this negates the required criminal intent (mens rea).

— People v. Photo (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 345
Systemic Failure: Resistance from a victim should not be misconstrued as being unwilling to participate. Repeatedly recounting an assault can be re-traumatizing. A victim's hesitance or disappearance may be a sign of crisis, not a lack of cooperation. Prosecutors should work with victim specialists and give the victim time and access to supportive services. — U.S. Department of Justice, Framework for Prosecutors (2024)
